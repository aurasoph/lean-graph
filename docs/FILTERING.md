# Dependency Filtering Design

## Core Philosophy

The goal of a dependency graph is to answer: **what mathematical knowledge is required to understand or verify this theorem?**

This is different from what Lean's proof terms actually reference. A simple proof of `a + b = b + a` might reference `Nat.rec`, `Eq.mpr`, `congrArg`, `HAdd.hAdd`, equation lemmas, `outParam`, and a dozen other kernel objects. None of those are what you would write in a math paper — the one thing you would write is whichever base case or lemma you actually appealed to.

The filtering pipeline is trying to approximate a conceptual dependency from a mechanical one. A constant should appear in the graph if and only if a mathematician would cite it — if its absence would leave the proof incomplete.

---

## Four Filter Categories

### 1. Compiler Artifacts

**What**: Equation lemmas (`Nat.add.eq_1`), match blocks, `.mk` constructors that share their parent's source position, `brecOn`/`casesOn`/`noConfusion` siblings.

**Why filtered**: If a human did not write it, it does not represent a mathematical idea. The check is `isExplicitAPI`: does this declaration have a unique source position that does not piggyback on its parent's? Anything without a source range, or sitting at the exact same cursor position as its parent in `declRangeExt`, was synthesized by the elaborator.

**Expansion behavior**: When a filtered compiler artifact is encountered during proof-dep graph construction, we look for real mathematical content inside it. An equation lemma for a recursive definition wraps the actual recursive call — we want that to surface.

### 2. Structural Plumbing

**What**: Auto-generated `A.toB` coercions from `extends` (e.g. `CommSemiring.toSemiring`, `RingHom.toMonoidHom`).

**Why filtered**: These are the implicit scaffolding of the algebraic hierarchy. When the elaborator writes `CommRing.toSemiring`, it is navigating the inheritance structure automatically — no human chose to invoke it as a mathematical step. Compare this to `AlgEquiv.toLinearMap`, which is a hand-written conversion function expressing a real mathematical relationship; that one stays.

The distinction for `A.toB` names: filter only when `B` is a transitive ancestor of `A` in the `extends` chain. This prevents over-filtering hand-written conversions where `B` is not actually in `A`'s hierarchy.

**Class projections**: All projection functions (field accessors auto-generated by the structure elaborator) are filtered — `HAdd.hAdd`, `Mul.mul`, `Norm.norm`, `CategoryTheory.CategoryStruct.comp`, `Quiver.Hom`, etc. Projections are notation-elaboration artifacts: `‖x‖` elaborates to `Norm.norm x`, `f ≫ g` elaborates to `CategoryStruct.comp f g`, but these are how Lean's typeclass machinery resolves syntax, not mathematical choices made by the proof author. Their bodies are pure field accesses (`fun inst => inst.fieldN`) with no content to surface, and the parent class/structure is an `inductInfo` excluded from proof deps. Filtered uniformly with no exceptions.

**Expansion behavior**: Projections are not expanded through — their bodies contain only field access infrastructure.

### 3. Typeclass Instances

**What**: Named instance declarations (`instAddNat`, `Set.instCompleteAtomicBooleanAlgebra`, etc.).

**Why filtered**: Instance proof bodies contain implementation-detail lemmas that are prerequisites of the instance itself, not of theorems that use it. When a theorem uses `Set.instCompleteAtomicBooleanAlgebra`, it is not conceptually invoking `le_sup_right` — that is an internal step in proving the instance, not in the theorem. If we expand through instance bodies, those internal lemmas leak into every theorem that uses the instance, through potentially long chains.

**Important caveat**: This filtering is *pragmatic*, not principled. Some instances represent substantive mathematical content — `Real.instField` asserts that ℝ is a field, which is a nontrivial theorem. Under this filter, such instances disappear from the graph by default. Use `--include-instances` to restore them.

**Expansion behavior**: Instances are not expanded through. The noise from doing so (internal lattice lemmas, etc.) outweighs any signal.

### 4. Tactic Internals

**What**: `Lean.Grind.*`, `Lean.Omega.*`, `Lean.RArray.*`, `Int.Linear.*`, `Nat.Linear.*`, `Nat.ToInt.*`, `Mathlib.Tactic.*`, `Mathlib.Meta.*`, `Std.Internal.*`, `Std.Tactic.*`, `Std.Sat.*`, `Aesop.*`, `Qq.*`.

**Why filtered**: This is *principled*, not pragmatic. When `omega` proves `a + b ≤ c`, the proof term references `Lean.Omega.LinearCombo.eval` and friends — but those are the implementation of the decision procedure, not mathematical facts being invoked. The theorem does not depend on omega's data structures; it depends on arithmetic being decidable. Unlike instances, there is genuinely no mathematical content to surface underneath these names.

**Expansion behavior**: Tactic internals are dropped entirely. We do not expand through them. Since the mathematical content is not underneath them — it is in the decision procedure itself — expansion would find nothing useful.

---

## Expansion Heuristic

When a filtered node is encountered in proof-dep graph construction, the default behavior is to look for real mathematical content inside it by opening its proof body and continuing the search. This is how equation lemmas and other compiler artifacts surface the actual recursive calls and lemmas they wrap.

The three exceptions are categories 2 (projections), 3 (instances), and 4 (tactic internals), all of which either have no mathematical content underneath or would produce worse signal-to-noise than simply dropping them.

---

## Filtering Tiers

**Exhaustive** (`--include-aux`): No filtering. Everything in the proof term appears.

**Standard** (default): Applies all four categories above.

---

## What This Filter Does Not Capture

The filter operates on Lean's proof terms, not on mathematical intent. Some things it cannot distinguish:

- A theorem that is proved by `rfl` still has `rfl` as a dependency — it appears as a node.
- Instances that represent genuine mathematical theorems (category 3 caveat above) are filtered by default.
- The filter cannot tell whether a call to a general lemma (like `le_antisymm`) was chosen deliberately or was found automatically by a tactic. Both look the same in the proof term.
